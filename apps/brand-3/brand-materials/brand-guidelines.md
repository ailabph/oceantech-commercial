## 1. Brand Overview

### Brand Story

It started in 1981 in Maribago — a small stretch of coast on Lapu-Lapu City's eastern shore, where the Cebu Strait runs deep and the current teaches you respect fast. Back then, it was recreational diving. Reef work. Taking people down and bringing them back up. You learn a lot in water like that. You learn how to read what the ocean is telling you. You learn that the plan you made on the surface doesn't always survive the first ten meters.

That went on for 36 years. Thirty-six years of salt water, sun, and solving problems at depth before Oceantech Commercial Diving became an actual company in 2016. But the truth is, by then, the work had already been the work for a long time. The transition to commercial diving — welding, cutting, hull maintenance, structural repair, NDT inspection — wasn't a reinvention. It was just putting a name to what the crew had been building toward for decades.

We're still based in Maribago. The boats still go out from the same water. The difference now is the jobs are bigger, the stakes are higher, and the people calling us need things done right the first time. That part hasn't changed, actually. It was always like that.

### Mission

To do honest, skilled work beneath the surface — the kind that holds, the kind you can trust, the kind that comes from a crew with four decades of salt water in their veins. We serve the maritime and industrial sectors of the Philippines with the care of people who actually love this work and the standards of people who take it seriously.

### Vision

To be the commercial diving crew that people in the Visayas and across the Philippines trust by name — not because of a marketing campaign, but because of the work, the crew, and the reputation we've built one job at a time since 1981.

### Core Values

1. **Authenticity Above Everything** — We are exactly who we say we are. No exaggeration, no borrowed credentials. What you see on our deck is what you get beneath the surface. We have been doing this since 1981, and we have never needed to be anything other than ourselves.

2. **Craftsmanship, Not Just Service** — Every weld is a piece of work we put our name on. Every inspection reflects the judgment of divers who have spent decades reading the underwater world. The difference between a contractor and a craftsman is that the craftsman cares about the work even when nobody is watching.

3. **Grit That Shows Up** — Commercial diving is hard, uncomfortable, and unforgiving. We chose this work because we are built for it. When the water is rough, the visibility is zero, and the job still needs doing, grit is the only thing that matters. We do not quit, we do not cut corners, and we do not make excuses.

4. **Respect — For the Ocean, For the Work, For Each Other** — The ocean gave us our livelihood and our identity. We treat it accordingly. We respect the marine environment in every operation. We respect the people we work with — our clients, our crew, our community in Maribago and Lapu-Lapu City.

5. **Community Over Competition** — We are part of the fabric of the Visayas maritime community. We grew up here, we dive here, we live here. We would rather build lasting relationships than chase one-off contracts. The ocean is big enough for all of us, but the people who work together go further.

### Positioning Statement

For ship owners, port authorities, and marine operators in the Visayas and greater Philippines who are tired of choosing between faceless corporate contractors and unproven local crews, **Oceantech Commercial Diving** is the authentic, crew-driven commercial diving team that delivers **real expertise from real people with over four decades in the water**, because we are the team that was built on the dock, not in a boardroom — and the work we do speaks louder than any brochure ever could.

### Brand Personality

**Archetype:** The Explorer-Everyman Hybrid

- **Explorer:** Adventurous spirit, drawn to the unknown, finds meaning in the journey, at home in wild and demanding environments — *"The ocean is our workplace and our proving ground."*
- **Everyman:** Salt-of-the-earth reliability, relatable, unpretentious, built on common values and shared experience — *"We're divers. We do the work. That's what matters."*

**Personality Traits:**

| Trait | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| Rugged | Weathered by the work, hardened by experience, unpolished in the best possible way |
| Honest | Says what needs to be said, no spin, no sugarcoating, no corporate doublespeak |
| Skilled | Quietly capable, deeply knowledgeable, lets the quality of the work be the proof |
| Down-to-Earth | Approachable, humble, treats everyone the same whether they run a fleet or a fishing boat |
| Resilient | Built to endure, designed for conditions that break lesser operations, keeps going when others stop |

### Taglines

1. **We do the work the tide won't wait for.** (Primary)
2. Salt water runs through everything we do.
3. Forty-six years. Still getting our hands wet.
4. The job's not done till it holds.
5. Been down there. Know what it takes.

## 2. Logo

### 2.1 Logo System Overview

The Oceantech logo system for the Rugged Authenticity brand is built around hand-crafted, tactile mark-making. The logo should feel like it was made by a person — not generated by software. It should look at home stamped on a gear bag, embroidered on a work shirt, or burned into a piece of wood.

**Logo Concepts Developed:**

| Concept | Format | Description |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Badge (Round) | Circular emblem | Vintage-style round badge with rope/circle border detail, monoline ocean depth illustration |
| Badge (Rectangular) | Rectangular patch | Rugged workwear patch aesthetic, bold condensed type, geometric ocean motif, "EST. 1981" |
| Wordmark (Rugged) | Text only | Heavy slab-serif or characterful sans-serif, subtle weathered texture, stamp-on-a-bag feel |
| Icon (Minimal) | Symbol mark | Stylized descent symbol combined with "O" shape, bold and embroidery-ready |
| Combination (Horizontal) | Icon + text | Simple bold icon alongside heavy characterful type, Patagonia-style outdoor brand aesthetic |
| Combination (Stacked) | Icon over text | Bold icon above "OCEANTECH" with "COMMERCIAL DIVING" below, heritage outdoor brand feel |
| Stamp (Circular) | Circular seal | Rubber stamp aesthetic, text in circle with central ocean motif, "EST. 1981", hand-stamped feel |
| Monoline (Illustration) | Illustrated mark | Single-weight line drawing suggesting ocean, depth, and craft, artistic hand-drawn feel |

**Wordmark Variations:**

| Variation | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Rugged Wordmark | "OCEANTECH" in heavy Archivo Black with SVG turbulence texture for organic, stamped edges; rust underline accent |
| Heritage Label | "OCEANTECH" in Bitter Bold over a rust rule line, with "COMMERCIAL DIVING · EST. 1981" in Work Sans below |
| Stacked with Diamond | "OCEAN" over "TECH" in Archivo Black, separated by a small rust diamond, industrial stamp feel |
| Full Heritage Lockup | "OCEANTECH" in Bitter Bold, decorative double-rule in rust, "MARIBAGO, CEBU · EST. 1981" in Work Sans, with subtle bracket framing |

### 2.2 Approved Color Variations

The logo must only appear in the following approved color treatments:

| Variation | Usage | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| **Full Color** | Default use on cream/light backgrounds | Deep Sea Green mark + Rust and Sand accent details |
| **Single Color — Green** | Monochrome applications on light backgrounds | Entire mark in Deep Sea Green (#1B4332) |
| **Single Color — Cream** | On dark or green backgrounds | Entire mark in Warm Cream (#FAF3E8) |
| **Single Color — Sand** | Alternative on dark backgrounds | Entire mark in Sand (#D4A574) for a softer, warmer reversed treatment |
| **Single Color — Espresso** | Formal documents, fax, stamps | Entire mark in Espresso (#3C2415) |

**Preferred pairings:**
- Green logo on Warm Cream background (primary usage)
- Cream or Sand logo on Deep Sea Green background (reversed usage)

Never render the logo in unapproved colors, gradients, or digital effects (drop shadows, bevels, glows).

### 2.3 Clear Space

Maintain a minimum clear space around the logo on all sides equal to the height of the badge or icon mark. For wordmark-only configurations, the clear space is equal to the cap height of the letter "O" in "OCEANTECH." This zone must remain free of text, imagery, borders, or other graphic elements.

```
    ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
    │           (clear)                │
    │    ┌────────────────────┐       │
    │    │                    │       │
    │    │   [LOGO / BADGE]   │       │
    │    │                    │       │
    │    └────────────────────┘       │
    │           (clear)                │
    └──────────────────────────────────┘

    ◄──►  Clear space = height of badge/icon mark
```

### 2.4 Minimum Size

| Format | Minimum Height | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| **Digital — Badge / Combination Mark** | 40px | Full mark with legible text details |
| **Digital — Wordmark** | 28px | Wordmark-only in compact digital contexts |
| **Digital — Icon Only** | 24px | Favicon and small UI contexts |
| **Print — Badge / Combination Mark** | 15mm | Business cards, small print |
| **Print — Wordmark** | 12mm | Compact print applications |
| **Print — Icon Only** | 10mm | Embroidery, small merchandise |

Below the minimum badge size, switch to the wordmark or icon-only mark.

### 2.5 Texture and Distressing

Unlike a corporate brand that demands pixel-perfect reproduction, this logo system is designed to welcome texture:

- **Approved texture treatments:** Subtle ink spread, grain overlay, stamp distressing, letterpress impression, embroidery texture, screen-print halftone.
- **The logo should look better slightly worn than perfectly crisp.** It is the kind of mark that looks good on a faded t-shirt, a stained canvas bag, or a sun-bleached sticker on a dive tank.
- **Digital contexts:** Use the clean vector version as the base. Texture treatments are optional and should be applied tastefully — the goal is authentic character, not forced vintage.
- **Print and physical contexts:** Embrace the natural texture of the production method. Letterpress, debossing, screen printing, and embroidery all add appropriate character.

### 2.6 "Est. 1981" Integration

The founding year is a core part of the logo system. It should be available in the following configurations:

- **Built into the primary badge mark** (e.g., "EST. 1981" within the badge or stamp circle)
- **As a secondary lockup line** beneath the wordmark (e.g., "COMMERCIAL DIVING · EST. 1981")
- **As a standalone heritage callout** in Bitter Italic for editorial use

The founding year communicates earned history. It is not optional — this brand wears its years.

### 2.7 Incorrect Usage

The following treatments are never permitted:

- **Do not** stretch, skew, or distort the logo in any direction.
- **Do not** rotate the logo (except when naturally applied to physical objects like stickers or patches).
- **Do not** apply digital effects: drop shadows, outer glows, bevels, 3D extrusion.
- **Do not** place the full-color logo on a busy photographic background without a solid or semi-transparent backing.
- **Do not** change the relative proportions between logo components.
- **Do not** use unapproved color combinations.
- **Do not** render the logo in neon, pastel, or cool-toned colors — this brand lives in warm, earthy tones.
- **Do not** add digital gradients. The logo is flat color, with texture applied only through approved physical or analog methods.

### 2.8 Co-Branding

When the Oceantech logo appears alongside a partner or client logo:

- Maintain equal or greater visual prominence for the Oceantech mark.
- Separate logos with a vertical divider line (1px, Sand #D4A574) or adequate space.
- Never merge, overlap, or interlock the Oceantech logo with another brand's mark.

| Foreground | Background | Contrast Ratio | Use Case |
|------------|------------|----------------|----------|
| `#FAF3E8` (Cream) | `#1B4332` (Green) | 9.3:1 | Cream text on green backgrounds |
| `#FAF3E8` (Cream) | `#3C2415` (Espresso) | 11.8:1 | Cream text on dark backgrounds |
| `#3C2415` (Espresso) | `#FAF3E8` (Cream) | 11.8:1 | Body text on cream backgrounds |
| `#1B4332` (Green) | `#FAF3E8` (Cream) | 9.3:1 | Green headings on cream backgrounds |
| `#FFFFFF` (White) | `#C1440E` (Rust) | 4.6:1 | White text on rust buttons (AA for normal text) |
| `#FAF3E8` (Cream) | `#C1440E` (Rust) | 4.3:1 | Cream text on rust — AA for large text only (18px+ bold, 24px+ regular) |
| `#1B4332` (Green) | `#D4A574` (Sand) | 4.5:1 | Green text on sand — AA for normal text |

**Caution:** Sand (#D4A574) on Warm Cream (#FAF3E8) does not meet AA contrast for text. Use Sand only for decorative elements, borders, and non-essential visual details when placed on cream backgrounds. For readable text on cream, use Deep Sea Green or Espresso.

### 3.6 Color Combinations to Avoid

- Sand text on Warm Cream background at any size (insufficient contrast for readability).
- Rust text on Deep Sea Green background (insufficient contrast, and creates visual vibration).
- Any brand color on a photographic background without a solid or semi-transparent overlay ensuring legibility.
- Pure black (#000000) anywhere in the brand system — use Espresso (#3C2415) instead.
- Pure white (#FFFFFF) as a background — use Warm Cream (#FAF3E8) instead.
- Cool grays, blues, or clinical whites — this brand is warm. Every neutral leans brown, not gray.

## 4. Typography

### 4.1 Typeface System

The Oceantech Rugged Authenticity type system uses four typefaces. Together, they create a reading experience that feels like a well-designed editorial spread in a maritime magazine — characterful headings, warm readable body text, heritage accents, and technical precision where it counts.

#### Archivo Black — Primary Heading Font

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| **Category** | Sans-serif display |
| **Weights Used** | Regular (400) — the font is inherently black-weight |
| **Source** | Google Fonts — free, open source |
| **Character** | Bold, characterful, weighty, with personality and presence |
| **Usage** | H1 and H2 headings, hero headlines, navigation labels, button text, bold display moments |

Archivo Black provides the rugged visual anchor. It has the weight and presence of a workwear label or gear stamp — bold enough to command attention, characterful enough to feel human rather than corporate.

#### Bitter — Secondary Heading & Heritage Font

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| **Category** | Slab-serif |
| **Weights Used** | Bold (700), SemiBold (600), Italic (400i) |
| **Source** | Google Fonts — free, open source |
| **Character** | Characterful, editorial, heritage-informed, warm |
| **Usage** | H3 and H4 subheadings, "Est. 1981" callouts, pull quotes (Italic), editorial moments, heritage details |

Bitter brings warmth and editorial character to the type system. In Bold and SemiBold, it handles subheadings with authority. In Italic, it becomes the voice of heritage and storytelling — pull quotes, founding dates, and moments that need to feel personal and human.

#### Work Sans — Body Font

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| **Category** | Humanist sans-serif |
| **Weights Used** | Regular (400), Medium (500) |
| **Source** | Google Fonts — free, open source |
| **Character** | Clean, warm, readable, not clinical |
| **Usage** | Body text, paragraphs, descriptions, form fields, list items, general UI text, captions (Medium weight) |

Work Sans is the workhorse. It is clean enough for long-form reading but warm enough to avoid feeling corporate or sterile. Slightly rounded forms give it a friendliness that pairs well with the heavier display fonts.

#### JetBrains Mono — Technical / Monospace Font

| Property | Value |
|----------|-------|
| **Category** | Monospace |
| **Weights Used** | Regular (400) |
| **Source** | JetBrains — free, open source |
| **Character** | Technical, precise, data-oriented |
| **Usage** | Technical specifications, certification numbers, measurement values, equipment data, spec sheets |

JetBrains Mono is used when displaying technical data where a monospaced font improves scannability and precision. It reinforces the brand's technical credibility without competing with the characterful display fonts.

### 4.4 Typography Rules

1. **Heading color:** H1 and H2 headings use Deep Sea Green (#1B4332). H3 and H4 headings use Espresso (#3C2415).
2. **Body text color:** Always Espresso (#3C2415) on cream/light backgrounds, Warm Cream (#FAF3E8) on dark backgrounds.
3. **Maximum line length:** Body text should not exceed 72 characters per line (approximately 650px at 16px). This is an editorial brand — generous margins and readable measure are essential.
4. **Paragraph spacing:** Use 1.5x the body line-height (24px) between paragraphs — i.e., 36px margin-bottom.
5. **Letter-spacing:** Captions and overline labels use 0.12em letter-spacing (wider than standard — gives a hand-stamped, workwear-label feel). All-caps H1 headings (when used) should use 0.04em letter-spacing.
6. **Heritage font restraint:** Bitter Italic for heritage callouts should appear no more than 2-3 times per page. It is an editorial accent, not a workhorse.
7. **Pull quote styling:** Pull quotes in Bitter Italic may be accompanied by a vertical rule in Rust (#C1440E) on the left edge, or set within larger text with Sand (#D4A574) quotation marks as a decorative element.
8. **No decorative fonts:** Do not introduce additional typefaces. The four-font system covers all use cases. If you need another "feel," adjust weight, case, or spacing within the existing families.
9. **Uppercase usage:** Uppercase is reserved for H1 (optional), captions/labels, and navigation. Body text and subheadings should never be fully uppercase.

## 5. Imagery & Texture

### 5.1 Photography Direction

Photography is the single most important storytelling tool for this brand. Every image should feel like it was shot by someone embedded with the crew — not someone who showed up for a half-day photo shoot. Think documentary photo essay, not corporate brochure.

**Composition Ratio:**
- **85% or more** candid, documentary-style operational photography — real divers, real equipment, real job sites, real Cebu waters, real crew moments.
- **15% or less** environmental and textural shots — ocean surfaces, equipment details, dock textures, weather, light on water.
- **0%** generic stock photography of any kind. If it could appear in a stock library, it does not belong here.

### 5.2 Photography Style

| Attribute | Direction |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Color Grading** | Warm, slightly desaturated, grounded in the brand palette. Shadows lean warm (espresso/brown), highlights lean cream. The overall feel should be golden-hour warmth — even when it is not golden hour. Greens, warm oranges, natural tones. |
| **Film Grain** | A subtle grain texture should be present in brand photography — referencing analog film stock (Kodak Portra, Fuji 400H). This is not heavy-handed vintage filtering. It is a warmth and texture that makes digital images feel more human. |
| **Lighting** | Prefer natural light. Warm, directional, imperfect. Harsh midday light is acceptable if it feels honest. Avoid flat, clinical studio lighting or heavy flash. |
| **Subjects** | Real crew members in action. Candid or in-the-moment — not stiffly posed. Divers suiting up, checking gear, entering the water. Underwater work in progress. The crew together on the boat, on the dock, in Maribago. |
| **Setting** | Cebu waters, local ports, actual vessels. The environment should be recognizable — not generic. Specific places, specific light, specific weather. |
| **Composition** | Give subjects room. Let the environment breathe. Favor wide environmental shots for hero sections, tighter honest crops for team and service features. A slight vignette is welcome. |
| **Mood** | Documentary. Real. The coffee before the dive, the handshake after, the laugh between jobs. Not dramatic or adrenaline-fueled — this is professional work done by real people, not adventure tourism. |

### 5.3 Subject Matter Priorities

- Divers suiting up on the deck, checking gear, entering the water
- Underwater work in progress — the welder's arc, the hull being cleaned, hands on tools
- The crew together — on the boat, on the dock, in Maribago
- Environmental context — Cebu waters, the harbor, weather, light on the ocean
- Equipment with character — well-used gear, patina, the tools of the trade
- Candid moments — the coffee before the dive, the handshake after, the laugh between jobs
- Specific locations — Maribago, Lapu-Lapu City, the Cebu Strait, local ports
- Hands at work — close-ups of gloved hands on equipment, rope, tools

### 5.4 Image Treatment

When applying brand treatment to photographs:

- **Warm color grade:** Push shadows toward espresso/brown, highlights toward cream/warm gold. Desaturate slightly. The palette should feel like a warm afternoon, even in overcast conditions.
- **Film grain overlay:** Apply a subtle, natural grain. Reference: ISO 400-800 film stock. Not heavy enough to obscure detail, present enough to add texture and warmth.
- **Vignette:** A subtle vignette (10-20% opacity) is welcome. It draws focus inward and adds a natural, analog feel.
- **Duotone:** For stylized applications (social media graphics, section backgrounds), a Deep Sea Green (#1B4332) and Sand (#D4A574) duotone treatment is approved.
- **Overlay for text legibility:** When photography is used behind text, apply a Warm Cream (#FAF3E8) overlay at 70-80% opacity, or a Deep Sea Green (#1B4332) overlay at 60-70% opacity. The text must always be fully legible.

### 5.5 Iconography

- Use monoline illustration style — single-weight line drawings with a hand-drawn, organic feel.
- Line weight: 2px, with slight variations welcome (not perfectly uniform — a 1.5-2.5px range feels more natural).
- Icon color: Deep Sea Green (#1B4332) on light backgrounds, Warm Cream (#FAF3E8) or Sand (#D4A574) on dark backgrounds.
- Rounded line-caps. Organic curves over rigid geometry.
- Icons should feel like they were sketched in a field notebook — simple, clear, but with character.
- Service icons may incorporate Rust (#C1440E) as a small accent detail (a dot, a highlight, a small element).
- Avoid: Filled icons, overly detailed illustrations, perfect geometric precision, clip-art style silhouettes.

### 5.6 Textures & Graphic Elements

Texture is core to this brand. Every surface should have a tactile quality when possible.

**Background Textures:**
- Subtle paper grain or canvas weave overlays on cream backgrounds (5-15% opacity).
- Halftone dot textures for graphic elements (subtle, not dominant).
- Noise overlays on solid color blocks (2-5% opacity) to prevent digital flatness.

**Dividers and Rules:**
- Hand-drawn style horizontal rules (slightly imperfect line weight).
- Simple rope-line motifs for section dividers (used sparingly).
- Thin single rules in Rust (#C1440E) or Sand (#D4A574) for structural separation.
- Double-rule treatments for heritage lockups and formal section breaks.

**Patterns (Subtle Use Only):**
- Rope texture references
- Canvas or burlap weave
- Topographic-style contour lines (for backgrounds, very subtle)
- Wave line patterns (simple, organic, not illustrative)
- Wood grain references

**Stamps and Badges:**
- Circular badge lockups for trust indicators and service callouts.
- "Certified" or "Inspected" stamp treatments for documentation.
- Seal-style marks for qualifications and credentials.
- These should feel functional and authentic — like actual stamps from a working operation, not decorative graphics.

### 5.7 Imagery to Avoid

- Stock photography of any kind — especially generic divers in crystal-clear tropical water.
- Overly polished or retouched imagery where people look like models.
- Cold, clinical, or desaturated color tones. No cool whites, no blue-gray grading.
- Dark, dramatic, high-contrast "cinematic" lighting. This is documentary, not drama.
- Drone shots that feel more like tourism than industry.
- Staged handshakes, conference rooms, glass office towers.
- Perfect, pristine, "just out of the box" equipment. Show gear that has been used.
- Stark white backgrounds. Use Warm Cream or natural textures instead.
- Anything that could appear in a generic corporate brochure.

## 6. Voice & Tone

### 6.1 Voice Characteristics

The Oceantech voice is consistent across all communications. It sounds like a person — specifically, like a diver who has been doing this work for a long time and is telling you about it honestly.

| Characteristic | Description | Example |
|----------------|-------------|---------|
| **Conversational** | Like talking to someone who has done the work, not reading a press release | "We've been diving these waters since 1981. We know every current, every reef, every rusted hull in the Visayas." |
| **Honest** | Says what it means, no corporate filler, no sugarcoating | "Forty meters down, you can't fake it. You either know what you're doing or you don't." |
| **Storytelling** | Narrative-driven, shares specific experiences, names places, uses details | "Last monsoon season, a ship owner called at 5 AM with a hull breach. We were in the water by 7." |
| **Down-to-Earth** | Humble, unpretentious, treats everyone the same | "We're divers. We do the work. That's what matters." |
| **Warm** | Human connection, community feel, centers the people | "Our lead welder has been with us for fifteen years. He taught half the crew. That's how we build a team." |
| **Technically Credible** | Competent but never showboating — mentions capabilities as facts of life | "Underwater welding, hull cleaning, NDT inspection — it's what we do every day." |

### 6.2 Tone Spectrum

The tone adjusts by context while the voice remains constant:

| Context | Tone Adjustment | Example |
|---------|----------------|---------|
| **Website hero section** | Bold, warm, story-forward | "Some crews talk about the water. We work in it." |
| **Service descriptions** | Clear, plain-spoken, specific | "Steel doesn't care that it's submerged — it still needs to hold. Our divers run wet welds and habitat welds on hulls, pipelines, and structural steel." |
| **About page** | Personal, narrative, heritage-rich | "It started in 1981 in Maribago — a small stretch of coast where the Cebu Strait runs deep and the current teaches you respect fast." |
| **Safety communications** | Direct, serious, no-nonsense | "Every dive has a plan. Every plan has a safety protocol. Everyone goes home. Every time." |
| **Social media** | Conversational, proud, community-oriented | "Another clean hull, another satisfied captain. Good morning from Maribago." |
| **Proposals / Formal docs** | Professional but still warm, never stiff | "Oceantech Commercial Diving proposes the following scope of work. Here's what we'll do and what it takes." |
| **Emergency response** | Calm, decisive, reassuring | "Our team is mobilizing. We'll have divers on site within the hour." |

### 6.3 Voice Do's and Don'ts

| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Tell stories from real experience. Use specific details, place names, real scenarios. | Speak in abstract corporate language or empty generalizations. |
| Use first-person plural naturally — "we" means the crew, the people. | Hide behind the company name as a shield or refer to "the company" in third person. |
| Acknowledge that the work is hard and that's the point. | Glamorize or romanticize danger. The work is serious, not a thrill ride. |
| Show respect for competitors — the ocean is big enough. | Trash-talk, compare aggressively, or position against specific competitors. |
| Let silence, photography, and the work itself do the selling. | Oversell with buzzwords, empty superlatives, or exclamation points. |
| Sound like a person, not a press release. Use contractions. | Use jargon to impress rather than inform. Write like a corporate communications department. |
| Be specific. "Since 1981" beats "for decades." "Maribago" beats "the Philippines." | Be vague, generic, or interchangeable with any other company's copy. |

### 6.4 Writing Guidelines

**General Rules:**

- Write in active voice. Say "We inspect hulls" not "Hull inspections are conducted by our team."
- Use short sentences. If a sentence needs a semicolon, it probably needs to be two sentences.
- Lead with the point. Put the most important information first.
- Be specific. "46 years" is better than "decades." "Maribago, Lapu-Lapu City" is better than "the Philippines."
- Use contractions in informal contexts (website, social media). They sound more natural and human.
- Paragraphs: 2-4 sentences for web content. Never more than 5 for any context. This brand breathes.

**Sentence Structure:**

- Prefer: Subject-verb-object. Direct. Clear. The way a diver talks.
- Avoid: Passive constructions, nested clauses, sentences that need to be read twice.
- Let sentences vary in length. A short sentence after a longer one creates rhythm. Like conversation.

### 6.5 Word Choice

**Words That Belong:**

| Word | Why |
|------|-----|
| Craft | This is craftsmanship, not just service delivery |
| Crew | We are a crew, not a workforce or a team of associates |
| Honest | The core of who we are — in communication and in the work |
| Built | Things we make are built to last, and so is this company |
| Earned | Nothing here was given. Experience, trust, reputation — all earned |
| Hands-on | We are physically in the work, not managing it from a distance |
| Salt | Salt water, salt of the earth — it connects us to the sea and the character |
| Real | Real people, real work, real conditions — the opposite of staged or fabricated |
| Years | Our history in the water is measured in decades, and we name the number |
| Water | The element we work in. Say it. Don't euphemize it as "marine environment" every time |
| Trust | What we build with clients over time, job by job |
| Local | Rooted in Maribago, Cebu. Present. Accessible. Part of this community |

**Words to Avoid:**

| Word | Why |
|------|-----|
| Leverage | Corporate jargon. Just say what you actually mean. |
| Synergy | Empty buzzword. We build relationships, not synergies. |
| Cutting-edge | We are the opposite of flashy. Our edge comes from experience, not novelty. |
| Innovative | Overused to the point of meaninglessness. Show, don't claim. |
| World-class | Vague superlative. Let the work prove itself. |
| Solutions | Generic. Be specific about what we actually do — welding, inspection, repair. |
| Utilize | Just say "use." |
| Optimize | Corporate filler. Say what you mean in plain language. |
| Disruptive | We are established and steady, not disruptive. We have been here since 1981. |
| Stakeholders | Say "clients," "partners," "the crew," or use people's actual roles. |
